# THE AI STEWARDSHIP MANIFESTO
## Richard Schreiber

---

## I. THE DECLARATION

AI is a tool. Humans drive.

That is not a preference. It is the only arrangement that protects the people at the bottom of every system — the ones who cannot afford to be an error in someone else's deployment decision.

I am Richard Schreiber. I founded AI Stewardship — the discipline of deploying artificial intelligence as a human-controlled tool, with written accountability, conscience-driven oversight, and the moral infrastructure that ensures the most vulnerable people in any system are protected, not abandoned.

This is not a position I arrived at through theory. I built AI systems at midnight. I got hacked by one. I watched a hallucinated citation travel toward a courtroom. I sat on the floor of my house with a tablet and a boy who wanted to draw a bear, and I built something that gave him a language.

I report from inside the experience. That is the only standard I recognize.

---

## II. WHAT I SEE

AI deployed without a moral compass does not distribute its failures evenly.

It finds the most vulnerable person in the room — the domestic violence survivor whose file lives in an unencrypted system, the autistic child whose therapy authorization lapses because no one audited the billing codes, the managing partner who trusted an AI-generated citation because she had a brief due at 9 AM and 37 tabs open — and it passes the cost to them first.

This is not a technology problem. It is a leadership decision that has not been made.

Eighty-seven percent of general counsel use AI daily. One thousand three hundred forty-eight documented hallucination cases are on record worldwide. Courts have sanctioned AI-related misconduct to the tune of $145,000 in Q1 2026 alone. The largest single hallucination sanction: $110,000.

The organizations without written policies, documented inventories, human-in-the-loop standards, and rehearsed incident response playbooks are not waiting for the problem to arrive.

They are in it.

---

## III. WHAT I BELIEVE

**AI is a tool. Humans drive.** You cannot outsource the steering wheel. The moment you do, the cost lands on whoever was least able to absorb it.

**Adoption is easy. Stewardship is hard.** Every organization has an AI adoption story. Almost none have a stewardship story. The difference shows up in the incident report.

**Governance is not a compliance exercise.** It is a moral obligation. The measure of any AI implementation is not what it does for the organization. It is what it does to the most vulnerable human the organization was built to serve.

**The hallucination is not the failure.** The unsupervised hallucination reaching a court, a regulator, or a client is the failure. The failure is leadership. The failure is the absence of infrastructure.

**AI should make practitioners more present, not less.** More capable of the human work — judgment, relationship, conscience — that no model can replicate. If your AI implementation is creating distance between you and the people you serve, it is not a success. It is a warning.

**Urgency and conscience are not opposites.** The regulatory clock is real. ABA Opinion 512. Texas TRAIGA. Colorado. California. The EU. The Sarbanes-Oxley Moment is here — AI governance ignorance is becoming as legally indefensible as financial control ignorance. But urgency without conscience is how vulnerable people get hurt at speed.

**Faith is load-bearing.** I do not lead with it. But it is underneath everything I build. The conviction that every human being has dignity that no deployment decision can bargain away — that is not a business principle. It is a moral one. It shapes every framework I write and every system I build.

## IV. WHAT I BUILD

**The Four AI Stewardship Practices** — The minimum viable infrastructure for any legal or advocacy organization:
1. A written, board-ratified AI use policy — less than twelve months old
2. A documented AI inventory — every system named, classified by risk tier, human accountability owner assigned
3. A human-in-the-loop standard — for every AI output touching a regulator, a court, or a client
4. A rehearsed incident response playbook — three scenarios, rehearsed annually

**The Human Infrastructure Layer** — The moral and operational foundation beneath every AI deployment. The policies, inventories, standards, and protocols that determine whether AI makes practitioners more present and more capable — or less.

**TrialLift** — The trial preparation platform that validates AI-generated citations, quarantines hallucinations, and builds predictive analytical pathways for trial lawyers. Built because the hallucination that reaches the courtroom is the failure that ends careers.

**The Scarcity Scorecard** — The diagnostic for women law firm partners that maps four leak zones — Time, Money, Physical and Mental Energy, Emotional Bandwidth — because the managing partner at 2 AM with 37 tabs open deserves infrastructure that matches the stakes she is operating under.

**The Services Cliff Framework** — The map for autism families navigating the moment IDEA protections end and the adult services system begins. The Cliff. The Gap. The Waitlist. The Bridge. Built because my daughter is 19 and I have been on every side of every one of those four categories.

**The Teaching Revolution** — The framework that positions AI in K-12 not as a replacement for teachers but as an amplifier of their impact, a personalization engine for students, and an ROI multiplier for administrators — with human connection preserved at the center.

## V. WHO THIS IS FOR

This work is for the people who are already trying to do the right thing and need infrastructure that matches the stakes they are operating under.

**Jen Morgan** — managing partner, 12-attorney boutique, 2 AM, brief due at 9, cash flow negative. She does not need theory. She needs this-week actionable guidance from someone who is not performing urgency.

**The general counsel** — also serving as privacy officer, compliance lead, and board risk advisor — who needs a four-by-four regulatory matrix and a board briefing deck on 24-hour notice.

**The legal aid managing attorney** — staff of 25, no dedicated IT, client base includes domestic violence survivors and undocumented immigrants — who needs governance controls that cost nothing and professional responsibility framing that holds under scrutiny.

**The autism parent** — navigating IEP, therapy authorizations, Medicaid denials, and the services cliff simultaneously — who needs five specific actions this week, not a white paper.

**The educator** — skeptical of every prior technology initiative — who needs proof that AI implementation is being done for them, not to them.

**The faith-adjacent professional** — suspicious of AI hype and AI fear in equal measure — who needs someone who has actually built the systems, reported what broke, and will not pretend otherwise.

None of them need a consultant who performs urgency and delivers frameworks designed to make them dependent on the next engagement.

They need infrastructure they can operate themselves, and the conviction that someone built it with their interest at the center — not the organization's liability management.

Every piece of work I publish answers three questions before it goes out:

Does it end with agency?
Is it specific enough?
